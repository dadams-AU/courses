Why Study Policy Actors?

Who makes policy decisions and why?

Understanding the motivations, constraints, and behaviors of policy actors helps us predict and explain policy outcomes.

Three Theoretical Approaches

How do we study policy actors? Three main frameworks:

Institutionalism

Focus on formal rules and structures

Behavioralism

Focus on individual motivation and actions

Neo-Institutionalism

Synthesis of both approaches

Institutionalism

Key Characteristics

  • Studies politics through formal institutional interactions
  • Focuses on official rules and procedures
  • Emphasizes relationships between institutions
  • Concerned with constitutional and legal frameworks

Historical Context

  • Dominant approach until mid-20th century
  • Primarily descriptive in nature
  • Focus on constitutional design and formal powers
  • Attention to official processes and relationships
Example: Studying how separation of powers affects policy development by analyzing formal constitutional provisions and institutional rules.

Behavioralism

Key Characteristics

  • Focuses on individual political motivation and behavior
  • Analyzes incentives and disincentives
  • Examines relationships between individuals and groups
  • Emphasizes empirical observation and data

Methodological Shift

  • Emerged in mid-20th century
  • Scientific approach to politics
  • Statistical analysis of political behavior
  • Focus on what actors actually do vs. formal roles
Example: Analyzing how reelection concerns influence legislators' voting patterns and policy positions regardless of formal institutional roles.

Neo-Institutionalism

Key Characteristics

  • Synthesizes institutional and behavioral approaches
  • Examines sociological aspects of institutions
  • Studies how institutions shape behavior
  • Applies rational choice theory to institutional analysis

Multiple Variants

  • Historical institutionalism
  • Rational choice institutionalism
  • Sociological institutionalism
  • Discursive institutionalism
Example: Analyzing how congressional committee structures create incentives for certain behaviors while constraining others, shaping both individual action and policy outcomes.

Comparing Theoretical Approaches

Approach Focus Primary Unit Key Question
Institutionalism Formal rules Institutions How do structures shape policy?
Behavioralism Individual actions People Why do actors behave as they do?
Neo-Institutionalism Interactive effects Both How do structures and behaviors influence each other?
Each approach provides valuable but incomplete insights into policy processes

Key Institutional Concepts

Institutional Inertia

The tendency of formal institutions to resist change

Like Newton's First Law: objects at rest tend to stay at rest

Path Dependency

Historical decisions shape and constrain current options

Past choices create "lock-in" effects

Punctuated Equilibrium

Long stability periods interrupted by rapid change

Most of the time: slow change. Sometimes: dramatic shifts
These concepts help explain why institutional change is difficult but not impossible

Types of Policy Actors

Official Actors

Formally recognized participants in the policy process with constitutional or legal authority

Primary Branches

  • Legislative Legislators
  • Executive President/Governors
  • Administrative Bureaucrats
  • Judicial Judges

Key Characteristics

  • Formal authority granted by constitution or law
  • Recognized role in policy process
  • Subject to checks and balances
  • Accountable to public (directly or indirectly)

Focus: Legislators

Why start with legislators?

In democratic systems, legislators are the primary lawmaking body with constitutional authority to create policy.

Legislative Functions

Primary Roles

  • Lawmaking authority
  • Budget and appropriations
  • Executive oversight
  • Constituent representation

Institutional Features

  • Committee structures
  • Parliamentary procedures
  • Party leadership systems
  • Bicameral negotiations
These formal roles and structures create the framework within which legislators operate

Legislative Motivations

The "Electoral Connection"

Understanding legislators' behavior through their need to secure re-election

Primary Goals

  • Re-election - staying in office
  • Policy - achieving preferred outcomes
  • Power - gaining influence in institution

Behavioral Implications

  • Constituent service activities
  • Position-taking on visible issues
  • Credit-claiming for benefits
  • Coalition building strategies

Legislative Constraints

Institutional Constraints

  • Committee jurisdiction limits
  • Parliamentary rules and procedures
  • Leadership agenda control
  • Bicameral approval requirements

Political Constraints

  • Party discipline expectations
  • Interest group pressure
  • Media attention and scrutiny
  • Electoral consequences
Neo-institutionalism helps explain how these formal constraints interact with behavioral incentives

What Legislators Actually Do

Lawmaking

  • Bill drafting and research
  • Coalition building
  • Committee markup
  • Floor voting

Oversight

  • Committee hearings
  • Agency investigations
  • Budget review
  • Program evaluation

Representation

  • Constituent services
  • District outreach
  • Stakeholder meetings
  • Media appearances

Analyzing Legislators: All Three Approaches

Approach Focus on Legislators Key Insights
Institutionalism Formal powers and procedures Committee structures, voting rules shape outcomes
Behavioralism Electoral motivations Re-election concerns drive position-taking
Neo-Institutionalism Rules + incentives interaction How institutional roles shape electoral strategies
The most complete understanding comes from combining all three perspectives

Case Study: Tax Reform Legislation

How do all three approaches help us understand legislative behavior?

Institutional Factors

  • Revenue bills must originate in House
  • Committee jurisdictions may overlap
  • Budget reconciliation procedures
  • Conference committee dynamics

Behavioral Factors

  • Constituency tax preferences
  • Campaign donor considerations
  • Leadership career ambitions
  • Ideological commitments
Neo-institutional insight: Formal rules create strategic opportunities, but electoral incentives determine which opportunities legislators pursue

Key Takeaways

  • Three theoretical lenses each reveal different aspects of legislative behavior
  • Institutional concepts like inertia and path dependency explain policy stability
  • Legislators operate at the intersection of formal rules and electoral incentives
  • Complete analysis requires attention to both structures and behaviors
